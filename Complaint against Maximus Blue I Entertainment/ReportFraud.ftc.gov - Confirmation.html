Your Next Steps
If you paid a scammer with a credit or debit card:
  • File a dispute (also called a “chargeback”) with your credit or debit card company. Tell them it was a fraudulent charge. Ask them to reverse the transaction and give you your money back.
    • Online: Log onto your credit or debit card company's website and go through the dispute process.
    • By phone: Call the phone number on the back of your card and tell them why you're filing a dispute.
  • Follow up with a letter to your credit or debit card company. To protect any rights you may have, follow up in writing by sending a letter to the address listed for billing disputes or errors. Use our sample letter.
  • Save your records. Keep any letters, notes, or emails related to the scam - they could help prove you're entitled to a refund if the credit or debit card company has any questions.
  • Find out your rights at ftc.gov/credit.
General Advice:
  • You can find advice and learn more about bad business practices and scams at consumer.ftc.gov.
  • If you’re concerned that someone might misuse your information, like your Social Security, credit card, or bank account number, go to IdentityTheft.gov for specific steps you can take.
  • Learn more about impersonation scams at ftc.gov/impersonators. If someone says they are with the FTC, know that the FTC will never demand money, make threats, tell you to transfer money, or promise you a prize.
  • You also can file a report with your state attorney general.
What Happens Next
  • Your report will help us in our efforts to protect all consumers. Thank You!
  • We can't resolve your individual report, but we use reports to investigate and bring cases against fraud, scams, and bad business practices.
  • We share your report with our law enforcement partners who also use reports to investigate and bring cases against fraud, scams, and bad business practices.
  • We use reports to spot trends, educate the public, and provide data about what is happening in your community. You can check out what is going on in your state and metro area by visiting ftc.gov/exploredata.
  • Investigations and cases do take time, but when we bring cases, we try to get money back for people. Check out ftc.gov/refunds to see recent FTC cases that resulted in refunds.

What happened

I am reporting a widespread, alarming pattern of organized financial fraud exploiting many individuals in Victoria, TX, often via Visa cards. My bank specialist confirmed this affects various local businesses, including Donut Palace (3602 Houston Hwy # J). VPD unfortunately views it as isolated, but it impacts many. On June 6, 2025, at 'Maximus' (1901 N Ben Wilson St, d/b/a 'Blue I Entertainment'), after a 21). Staff immediately took both my signed receipt and copy. I overheard them discussing forging tips for public exploitation. On June 8, 2025, my debit card was charged $32.83 by 'Blue I Entertainment' and an 'unauthorized $10 tip.' The 2-day delay was suspicious. Leaving, the bouncer threatened a $40 charge next time if I didn't tip, a clear act of extortion. This incident shows intentional fraud exploiting a first-time patron via illegal practices like name discrepancies, receipt alteration, delayed processing, and bouncer threats. Crimes/Violations by 'Blue I Entertainment'/'Maximus': Debit Card Fraud: Unauthorized $10 tip. Forgery: Receipt alteration, overheard plan. Theft by Deception: Stealing funds via false pretenses. Extortion: Bouncer's threat. DTPA Violation: Deceptive commercial acts. Conspiracy to Commit Fraud: Multiple staff involved. Indications of Organized Criminal Behavior: Systematic Operation: Premeditated exploitation discussion. Intentional Concealment: Receipt taken. Delayed Processing: Obscure fraud. Intimidation/Coercion: Bouncer's threat. Targeting Patrons: First-time exploitation. Discrepant Business Names: Confusion tactic. Broader Regional Pattern: A TDECU specialist confirmed fraud at Donut Palace and other 'shady' businesses, suggesting a wider network. Normalization/Lack of Local Investigation: VPD dismissal, state reluctance. My TDECU card issues (declined; fraud affidavit denial despite EMV chip protecting against counterfeiting, not all fraud) further highlight this systemic issue. I attempted to notify local law enforcement, but state reluctance to investigate widespread financial crimes is evident; I'm new to analyzing this, and they seem ill-equipped. I urge thorough investigations into 'Blue I Entertainment'/'Maximus' and this rampant organized criminal fraud in Victoria. A dedicated local system is vital, as current approaches fail. We need more detection, analysis, and awareness for banking members. This affects numerous consumers and demands immediate action.
